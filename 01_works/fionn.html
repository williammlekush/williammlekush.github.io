Fionn
February-May 2019

Movement forms the center of our humanity. The capabilities and traumas of our lives embed themselves in the systems of our body. Unfortunately, modern environments anesthetize and regulate our bodily expression, forcing all of us to emulate incapable, spiritless rulers. Most of us spend our days sitting in furniture based upon the idea of a throne, misusing a design optimized for lounging. Otherwise, we walk forward, lay down, and maybe spend an hour doing something “active.” Modern user-consumer citizens rarely carry our childhood capacity for a deep squat into adulthood as calves shorten, pelvises tilt, hamstrings and glutes tighten, and spines trade the evolutionary S-shape for a sedentary C-curve. Indeed, the environments in which we learn, live, and work discipline our bodies instead of supporting or expanding them. In response to this modern environment, we are often instructed to undergo extensive long-term rehabilitation in the form of strength, flexibility, and conditioning exercises, or devote our lives to a movement discipline. Out of collaboration with office workers, martial artists, and contemporary dancers, Fionn enters into this conversation as a suggestion that we should design our environments for our bodies, not against them.

Concept

Named after the legendary Irish giant who built the Giant’s Causeway, Fionn evokes the stability of those basalt columns by imimtating their hexagonal block form. The raw plywood and stripped aesthetic reference the simple implements used by hand-balancers, tracerus, and gymnasts. In these ways, the stool steps out of the way of the user, allowing them to change postures, personalize the aesthetic, and practice movement skills, such as the handstand.

Mimicking the aesthetic and form, the structural system focuses entirely on supporting the functions of the body. The wide surface area enables the user to securely rest in a variety of sitting and squatting positions. An internal cleat structure complemented by ergonomic handholds allow the height to be adjusted to access desks, tables, and workbenches without compromising the security of the structure. Finally, modular hexagonal shape allows for different combinations and arrangements of multiple stools.
